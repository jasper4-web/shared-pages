Why the business that answers first usually wins

It isn't the best price that wins most local jobs. It isn't even the best reputation. More often than anyone likes to admit, it's whoever picked up the phone.

What the research actually says

The most-cited figures come from the Lead Response Management study (around 2007) by Dr. James Oldroyd — vendor-sponsored research (InsideSales.com) on business-to-business web leads, so treat it as directional rather than peer-reviewed. It looked at three years of data across six companies — more than fifteen thousand leads and over a hundred thousand call attempts. The finding that made it famous: the odds of qualifying a lead drop about 21× when you call at 30 minutes instead of 5. The odds of making contact at all drop roughly 100×.

Harvard Business Review reported the same pattern from two angles. In a study of 1.25 million sales leads across 42 companies, firms that made contact within an hour were nearly seven times more likely to qualify the lead than firms that responded even an hour later — and more than sixty times more likely than those who waited a day or more.

Separately, the same authors audited 2,241 U.S. companies by submitting test inquiries and timing the reply. Only 37% responded within an hour, 23% never responded at all, and among those who did reply the average took about 42 hours. The bar is on the floor. You don't need to be brilliant — you need to be first.

You are not competing against perfect. You're competing against voicemail.

Why good businesses lose these jobs anyway

None of this is a character flaw. If you run a service business, the reason you miss calls is that you're doing the actual work:

  • You're under a sink, on a roof, or in an attic with both hands busy.
  • You're with a customer, and picking up would be rude.
  • It's 7pm, or Saturday, and you're finally sitting down.

Meanwhile the person calling you has a problem right now. They aren't running a careful evaluation of local providers. They're going down a list until somebody answers.

What "answering fast" really has to mean

Speed alone isn't the whole thing. A fast response that doesn't move the job forward just delays the same problem. To actually win the work, a first response has to do four things:

  • Happen in seconds, not hours — including nights and weekends.
  • Answer the real question — do you handle this, do you cover my area, roughly what does it cost.
  • Move to a time — an actual slot on an actual calendar, not "someone will get back to you."
  • Not need you — because if it needs you, it won't happen on the day you're busiest.

The three ways owners try to fix it

Answer it yourself, faster. This works until the week gets busy — which is exactly when the most calls come in. It also costs you the thing you were trying to protect: your attention.

Hire someone. A front-desk person solves it during business hours, five days a week, one call at a time. That's real money for partial coverage, and the calls that hurt most are often the after-hours ones.

Have something answer for you. The bar here is high: it has to sound like a person, know your business, book onto your real calendar, and hand anything unusual straight to you. When it clears that bar, it covers the hours a person can't and the moments when three people call at once.

The honest takeaway

You don't need to respond in five minutes to every lead forever. You need to stop losing the ones where you were the right business and simply weren't reachable. That's the gap — and it's usually the lowest-cost growth available to a local business, because you already paid to make that phone ring.
