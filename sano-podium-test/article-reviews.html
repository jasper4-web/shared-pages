How local businesses actually end up with more reviews

Almost every owner knows reviews matter. Very few have a system for getting them — which is why the shop with better reviews often isn't the one doing better work.

Why it's worth caring

BrightLocal's long-running Local Consumer Review Survey has consistently found that around 93% of consumers read online reviews before choosing a local business, and a large share read them regularly while browsing. For a business that shows up in local search, your rating is doing the selling before anyone talks to you.

The uncomfortable version: if a competitor with average work has sixty reviews and you have nine, most people never find out you're better.

Reviews aren't a reward for good work. They're a byproduct of asking — consistently, at the right moment.

The real reason you don't have more

It's almost never that customers were unhappy. It's that nobody asked, or the ask happened at the wrong time:

  • You asked while packing up, they said "sure," and then life happened.
  • You meant to text them that evening and forgot — reasonably, because you were working.
  • You asked a week later, when the relief of the fix had already faded.
  • You sent a link that took four taps to get to the right place.

What a system looks like

The businesses that quietly build a strong rating almost always do the same five things:

  • Ask every single time. Not the happy ones, not the big jobs — every completed job. Consistency beats selectivity.
  • Ask fast. The window where someone feels genuinely grateful is short. Same day beats next week.
  • Ask by text. Email is easy to ignore. A short text with a direct link is not.
  • Make it one tap. Every extra step loses people. The link should land exactly where they leave the review.
  • Catch problems privately first. If someone had a bad experience, you want to hear it before the internet does — and you want the chance to fix it.

What not to do

Don't offer anything in exchange for a review — discounts, entries, freebies. It violates the major platforms' policies, it can get reviews removed or your listing penalized, and in the US it can create real legal exposure. Ask plainly and let the work speak.

Also, don't only ask your favorite customers. Cherry-picking produces a slow trickle and a rating that never reflects your actual volume of good work.

The compounding part

Reviews are one of the few things in a local business that compound. A steady rating pulls in more calls, more calls mean more completed jobs, and more completed jobs mean more chances to ask. The hard part is never the asking — it's doing it every time, forever, on your busiest days. That's exactly the kind of thing that should not depend on a human remembering.

Sources: BrightLocal, Local Consumer Review Survey (annual) — share of consumers reading online reviews for local businesses. Platform policy guidance on review incentives from Google and the FTC. Figures are industry research, not SANO client results.
