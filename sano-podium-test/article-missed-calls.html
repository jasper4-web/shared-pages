Missed calls · 5 min read

What really happens when your phone goes to voicemail

Every owner knows they miss calls. Almost nobody knows how many, or what happens next. The answer to both is worse than most people guess.

Most calls aren't answered by a person

One widely-cited audit by 411 Locals placed calls to 85 businesses across 58 industries and tracked what happened. Only about 38% were answered by a live person. The rest went to voicemail or simply rang out. It's a vendor study rather than peer-reviewed research, so treat the exact figure as directional — but the direction is not really in dispute, and Harvard Business Review's audit of 2,241 companies found 23% never responded to a web enquiry at all.

Put plainly: for a typical small business, roughly three in five callers don't reach a human being.

The call you didn't hear is not a call you get to try again. It's a customer standing in a hallway with a phone, moving to the next name.

The voicemail assumption is the expensive part

The comforting story is: "If it matters, they'll leave a message." In practice, most people don't. Reaching a recording is a signal — it tells the caller the business is busy, closed, or hard to reach — and their problem hasn't gone away. So they hang up and keep dialing.

This is why missed calls are so much more costly than they look on a phone bill. A missed call isn't a delayed conversation. It's usually a conversation that happens somewhere else.

Where the misses actually cluster

They're rarely spread evenly. In most service businesses they bunch up in four predictable places:

  • While you're working. Hands busy, phone across the yard, or you simply can't hear it.
  • After hours and weekends. A meaningful share of urgent calls land when no office is open.
  • During the rush. Heat wave, freeze, storm — the day the phone matters most is the day you can't get to it.
  • The second and third caller. One line, one person. Two more people get a busy signal.

What actually closes the gap

You have four realistic options, and it's worth being honest about each.

Call them back faster. Better than nothing, but it depends on you noticing — and the whole problem is that you were busy.

An answering service. A human takes a message. Better than voicemail, but it usually can't answer real questions about your services or put a job on your calendar, so you still have to do the work later.

Hire a front desk. Excellent during business hours. It doesn't cover evenings and weekends, it's one call at a time, and it's a real salary.

Automatic text-back plus a real answering system. The lowest-effort fix that actually works: any call you can't take triggers an immediate text so the customer knows they were heard, and a system that can answer common questions and book the appointment handles the rest.

A simple test

Call your own business number tonight at 7pm from a number nobody recognizes. Then call it on Saturday morning. Whatever happens is what happens to every new customer who tries you outside business hours.

Most owners are surprised. That surprise is the opportunity — it's usually the lowest-cost revenue you'll find, because you already spent the money that made the phone ring.

Sources: 411 Locals call-answering audit — 85 businesses across 58 industries (vendor study, directional). Harvard Business Review, “The Short Life of Online Sales Leads” (Oldroyd, McElheran & Elkington, 2011) — audit of 2,241 U.S. companies. Figures are industry research, not SANO client results.
