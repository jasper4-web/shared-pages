The blueprint we'd actually hand you

This is a real sample of our own work product — the HVAC blueprint an owner would read and sign off before a single customer hears anything. It isn't a customer result and it isn't a case study. It's the thing you'd approve. Yours is written for your trade — this is the HVAC one.

How to read this: everything below is what we write for you. You mark it up, we change it, and nothing goes live until you say the words are right.

1. How your phone gets answered

The greeting, verbatim, in both languages:

  • English: "Thanks for calling [Company]. This is an assistant on their team — I can book you in or get a tech out. What's going on with your system?"
  • Spanish: "Gracias por llamar a [Company]. Soy un asistente del equipo. ¿Qué está pasando con su sistema?"

It identifies itself as an assistant at the start of every call, and announces that the call is recorded. Both are non-negotiable and both are in your approved script.

2. What counts as an emergency

Your rules, not ours. The default we'd propose for HVAC:

  • Same-day: no cooling and someone at home is elderly, an infant, or unwell; no cooling with an indoor temperature above 85°F; any burning smell or visible water damage.
  • Next available: weak airflow, one room not cooling, noise complaints, thermostat trouble.
  • Quote & schedule: replacement inquiries, second opinions, new-construction work.

Anything in the first bucket rings your cell within 60 seconds with the address and the symptom already captured.

3. What it will and won't say

The boundary is explicit, because this is the question every owner asks:

  • It will: confirm your service area, describe your services, give your diagnostic fee if you want it published, book against your real calendar, and take the details.
  • It won't: quote a repair price, promise a specific technician, diagnose over the phone, or commit to a time you haven't made available. Any of those become "let me have [Owner] confirm that" plus an immediate text to you.

4. What goes out after the job

  • A review request to every completed job — the same ask, the same public link, whoever they are.
  • If someone tells us they weren't happy, you hear about it the same day, and they still get the same link.
  • Maintenance-agreement renewal reminders at 11 months, then 11.5.

5. Your year, built in

  • March–April: tune-up campaign to last year's customers, before the rush.
  • May–September: peak. Answering and booking only — no campaigns competing for your capacity.
  • October–November: heater check reminders.
  • December–February: renewals, dormant-customer reactivation, referral asks.

6. What you approve before launch

A four-item checklist, signed by you: the greeting wording, the emergency rules, the price boundary, and the review copy. We don't launch on a "sounds good" — we launch on a signed sheet, because it's your name on the phone.
